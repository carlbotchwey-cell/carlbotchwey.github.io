Working Papers

1. Market Reaction to Earnings Announcements News of Cross-Listings: A Comparative Analysis of European Firms and Their Sponsored ADRs

(Carl Botchwey and François Aubert)
Under review at Review of Quantitative Finance and Accounting.

Abstract: This study investigates the asymmetric market responses to earnings announcements by comparing European-listed firms with their sponsored American Depositary Receipts (ADRs). Leveraging a novel paired-sample design, we control for firm fundamentals to isolate the effect of institutional and regulatory environments on investor behavior. Our analysis focuses on earnings surprise and earnings management and their impact on cumulative market-adjusted returns (CMAR) over the earnings announcement window.

ADRs elicit significantly stronger positive reactions to earnings surprises and more severe penalizations for earnings management relative to their European counterparts. Our results are robust to accounting standard alignment (IFRS and U.S. GAAP). The study reveals that differences in market reaction stem primarily from investor composition, legal enforcement and disclosure environment. In addition, it provides empirical support for both the Bonding Hypothesis and Investor Recognition Hypothesis. It highlights the role of U.S. institutional frameworks in amplifying investor sensitivity to earnings quality. Our results carry implications for cross-listed firms, global investors, and policymakers aiming to harmonize financial reporting and governance standards across capital markets.

2. Pre-disclosure Information Asymmetry and Cross-Listings

(Carl Botchwey and François Aubert)

Abstract: This study examines whether listing venue influences financial analysts’ forecasting behavior by comparing U.S.-listed sponsored American Depositary Receipts (ADRs) with the European-listed shares of the same firm. To isolate institutional effects from firm-specific heterogeneity, we employ a within-firm paired design that analyses analyst forecasts for identical firm-year observations across listing venues.

The results indicate that analyst forecasts associated with ADRs exhibit modestly higher forecast accuracy and substantially lower forecast disagreement than forecasts associated with the corresponding European listings. Both forecast range and forecast dispersion are lower for ADRs, indicating stronger convergence in analyst expectations within the U.S. cross-listed environment.

Overall, the results indicate that U.S. cross-listing is associated with modest improvements in forecast accuracy and substantially greater consensus among analysts. However, the evidence does not support the view that lower disagreement necessarily reflects a superior information environment. By distinguishing between forecast accuracy, forecast disagreement, and the underlying structure of analyst information, the study contributes to the cross-listing literature and highlights the importance of separating informational convergence from behavioural convergence when evaluating the informational consequences of cross-listing.
